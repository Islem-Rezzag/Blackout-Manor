# Art Bible V2

## Product Vision

Blackout Manor should feel like a polished cinematic social-deduction broadcast set inside one storm-locked gothic estate. A first-time viewer should understand the house, the suspects, the current phase, and the public evidence conflict without reading a dashboard.

The runtime target is a premium 2.5D gothic manor game:

- a connected cutaway house with readable rooms, doors, corridors, and threshold travel
- warm inhabited interiors under cold storm pressure
- masked human suspects with theatrical but grounded silhouettes
- physical meetings around a long dining table
- public claim, evidence, alibi, contradiction, and vote surfaces that support Prompt 3 without exposing hidden cognition
- broadcast-quality HUD language that belongs to the manor fiction instead of generic web analytics

This packet is not permission to modify runtime presentation code. It defines the production direction future implementation branches should follow.

## Visual Thesis

An amber-lit gothic cutaway manor under blue storm light, staged like a live mystery broadcast with human suspects, readable public evidence, and restrained premium overlays.

## Visual Pillars

### One Connected Estate

The manor must read as one designed house. Rooms are not cards. Corridors, doors, stair thresholds, and service routes should explain travel before labels do.

### Warm Interior, Cold Exterior

Interior lamps, candles, fireplaces, brass, and polished wood carry the human drama. Windows, roofs, rain, moonlight, surveillance feeds, and exterior edges carry the cold storm threat.

### Human Masquerade Cast

Suspects are stylized realistic people in formal storm-night masquerade attire. Their bodies, clothing, masks, accessories, and posture should carry identity before color or labels do.

### Evidence Is Public Theater

Evidence, claims, contradictions, alibis, votes, and bodies should appear as public artifacts on the broadcast surface and in the world. They should never reveal hidden role analytics or private chain-of-thought.

### Clarity Beats Decoration

Suspense should never hide gameplay-critical information. Darkness, fog, lightning, ornate furniture, and UI treatment must preserve task, door, body, agent, and claim readability.

## Camera Model

### Whole-Manor Overview

The default view is a wide cutaway composition with the Grand Hall as the orientation anchor. The viewer should understand:

- which rooms are occupied
- where travel can happen next
- where a body, sabotage, clue, or meeting destination is located
- how social rooms connect to service and technical rooms

### Room Focus

Room focus is a medium zoom, not a separate scene. It should preserve at least one adjacent threshold or corridor edge when possible. Focus mode is for:

- task interactions
- body discovery
- accusations and reassurance
- inspection of claim/evidence context
- meeting arrival and seating details

### Surveillance Mode

Surveillance mode is an in-world console layer. It should feel colder, flatter, scan-lined, and more technical than direct observation, but it must still use the same room silhouettes and public state. It is not a separate website.

### Event Overrides

Reports, sabotage, meetings, contradiction reveals, and vote outcomes may briefly override passive camera focus. These overrides should be short and reversible. They may dramatize the moment, but they must not hide nearby public evidence or agent positions.

## Composition Rules

- Anchor the house on the Grand Hall, with the dining/meeting room, library, study, kitchen, ballroom, greenhouse, surveillance room, generator room, cellar, and service corridor reading as connected wings.
- Give every major room one dominant silhouette visible from whole-manor scale.
- Keep clear negative space around doors, body positions, task approach lanes, and meeting chairs.
- Use room labels as support, not as the only way to identify a room.
- Avoid evenly spaced room modules. The estate should have wings, mass, hierarchy, and architectural asymmetry.
- Avoid nested UI cards over the game view. Use broadcast plates, strips, lenses, and thin framed overlays.
- Keep the first live frame game-first: manor, agents, and public phase should dominate; host-page chrome should not.

## Lighting Rules

- Interior key: amber, candle-gold, brass, fireplace orange, oil-lamp halos.
- Exterior key: slate blue, rain silver, moon gray, lightning white-blue.
- Blackouts should remove confidence from the room, not erase readable silhouettes.
- Lightning should briefly reveal windows, wet roof edges, suspect profiles, reflective floor details, and body silhouettes.
- Evidence highlights use restrained warm gold or paper-white, not arcade neon.
- Contradiction alerts use claret, hot amber, and sharp white edge light. They should feel like a broadcast alarm, not a sci-fi warning panel.
- Meeting lighting pools around the dining table, chairs, and speaker focus. The storm remains visible around the perimeter.
- Surveillance lighting is colder and lower contrast, but it must keep room geometry, occupants, and public markers legible.

## Color And Material Language

### Core Palette

- Warm manor: candle gold, aged brass, walnut, dark oak, burgundy velvet, muted claret, parchment, warm marble.
- Cold storm: slate blue, moon gray, rain silver, desaturated navy, wet stone, black glass.
- Social accents: deep jewel tones, forest green, wine red, dark teal, tarnished gold, muted violet.
- Danger accents: claret, ember, low red practical lights, warm-white shock flashes.
- Evidence accents: parchment, brass pin, wax seal red, muted gold linework, white chalk.

### Materials

- Floors: parquet, marble medallion, kitchen tile, greenhouse stone, cellar flagstone, service concrete, industrial metal.
- Walls: carved wood paneling, bookshelves, wallpaper, stone, glazed conservatory frames, boiler brick, technical paneling.
- Props: brass, paper, leather, velvet, iron, glass, ceramic, coal, steam pipe, rain-wet stone.
- UI: dark lacquered metal, smoked glass, brass hairlines, parchment inserts, broadcast scan overlays.

### Avoid

- plastic panels
- sci-fi blues as the dominant world language
- flat toy-room colors
- pure black shadows that hide state
- generic fantasy tavern brown everywhere
- neon arcade outlines on core gameplay objects

## Room Readability Rules

- Every room needs one hero prop cluster visible from whole-manor mode.
- Every room needs one clean path lane for agents.
- Every room needs one obvious interaction side for its main task hotspot.
- Every door or threshold must remain visible before and after a character crosses it.
- Bodies must be visible as human silhouettes on the floor, not lost among props or shadows.
- Cutaway walls should reveal room identity while keeping playable floor readable.
- Secondary dressing should enrich the fiction without crowding task points or meeting paths.
- Whole-manor mode can simplify secondary clutter; room focus can reveal more texture and prop richness.

## Social Deduction Readability Rules

- Suspects must be distinguishable by silhouette, posture, mask/accessory, and movement at whole-manor scale.
- Public speech and claims should connect visually to the speaker, room, and evidence references.
- Evidence should appear as cited public artifacts, not as omniscient truth.
- Contradictions should connect claim-to-claim or claim-to-evidence with a visible public line, stamp, or alert path.
- Alibis should appear as public timeline segments with uncertainty states, not as hidden-role proof.
- Votes should feel like table theater: seat, portrait, public ballot state, and outcome staging align.
- Dead, missing, disconnected, or seated states should be readable without relying only on text.

## Horror And Suspense Rules

The game can be tense, but never unreadable.

- Use storm, candle flicker, creaking architecture, and long shadows for mood.
- Keep pathing lanes brighter than non-interactive corners.
- Keep bodies, doors, active tasks, and speaking suspects above the darkness floor.
- Use camera linger to build dread, not to withhold public information.
- Avoid jump-scare UI, horror gore, extreme darkness, or visual effects that make spectators miss public actions.
- Fear should read through posture, spacing, portrait expression, and social reactions rather than gore.

## Concept Image Guidance

The concept image is the north-star for:

- connected gothic cutaway composition
- Grand Hall hierarchy
- warm interior versus cold storm contrast
- readable social, service, and technical room identities
- masked human suspects in formal attire
- restrained event board and bottom broadcast strip
- physical dining-room meeting destination

It is reference-only for:

- exact room coordinates
- exact crop
- exact painterly finish
- exact cast designs
- exact object count
- exact UI placement

Future art should adapt the concept through the floorplan and production docs. It should not trace, copy, or treat the image as a final runtime asset.

## Evidence, Claims, Contradictions, Alibis, And Votes

### Evidence

Evidence appears as public, citable objects. Use parchment cards, brass pins, wax seals, room icons, and small source tags. Evidence visuals should show what was observed, where, when, and by whom if that is public.

Support levels should be visually distinct:

- supported: warm gold seal, steady line, readable source tag
- contested: split gold/claret mark, diagonal tension line, unresolved tag
- contradicted: claret stamp, sharp edge light, linked counterclaim
- unverified: gray parchment edge, dashed line, low-contrast source tag

### Claims

Claims are speaker-bound public statements. They should show:

- claimant portrait or table seat
- short claim text or summarized public assertion
- room/time reference when available
- evidence refs when cited
- support level
- contradiction refs when present

Claims should not look like engine truth. They are social objects.

### Contradictions

Contradictions should land as public tension. Use a brief alert plate, claim-to-claim connector, and evidence chip stack. Avoid excessive flashing. The key read is "these public statements conflict," not "the game is revealing a hidden role."

### Alibis

Alibis live on a time-and-room strip. Use room silhouettes, tick/phase markers, public witness pips, and uncertainty states. The strip should distinguish witnessed, claimed, contested, and unknown intervals.

### Votes

Votes should be staged around the dining table. The UI can summarize ballot state, but the primary feel is seated suspects, portraits, public countdown, ballot tokens, and a final outcome reveal. Do not use hidden-role coloration before the game publicly reveals it.

## Live Mode Versus Replay And Dev Analytics

### Live Mode

Live mode is a full-screen game broadcast. It may show:

- phase label and timer
- room status
- compact event board
- cinematic subtitle strip
- public claims and evidence references
- alibi and contradiction surfaces when publicly relevant
- vote status and public outcome
- surveillance console

Live mode must not show:

- hidden roles before reveal
- private reasoning
- private chain-of-thought
- benchmark charts
- fairness analytics
- dev controls
- model internals
- contributor chrome

### Replay Mode

Replay mode can add scrubbing, event indexing, comparison, and more explicit source trails. It still must treat private cognition as out of bounds. Replay overlays should look like archive/broadcast analysis, not a generic data dashboard.

### Dev Analytics

Dev analytics and fairness views stay outside the live route. They may use tables and charts, but those surfaces should not define live match style.

## Explicit Privacy Rule

Never show private chain-of-thought in UI, logs, analytics, replay exports, tooltips, source ledgers, debug overlays, generated art briefs, or implementation comments. Public surfaces should show actions, evidence, claims, contradictions, alibis, votes, outcomes, and concise public summaries only.

## Do And Do Not Examples

### Do

- Show a gold evidence chip connected to a public claim from Iris.
- Show a claret contradiction alert when Victor's public alibi conflicts with a witnessed room transition.
- Keep the body silhouette visible under storm-darkened light.
- Make the Dining Room table and ten chairs readable before vote UI appears.
- Use surveillance feeds that reuse the room plates and public occupant markers.
- Use handcrafted room plate layers routed through a manifest.

### Do Not

- Do not add a React dashboard over `/game/[roomId]` as the main experience.
- Do not show "agent reasoning," "model thought," or hidden-role analytics in live HUD.
- Do not make rooms isolated floating cards.
- Do not use capsule or bean-shaped suspects.
- Do not copy Among Us visual language, UI iconography, or character shapes.
- Do not bury task hotspots under decorative clutter.
- Do not import third-party art without source and license review.
- Do not modify Phaser rendering in an art-direction docs branch.

## Implementation Handoff Principles

- Keep runtime code in `packages/client-game`.
- Keep authored data in `packages/content` or the appropriate manifest/source surface.
- Keep `apps/web` as the shell that mounts the runtime.
- Replace art through manifest and source metadata rather than ad hoc imports.
- Preserve deterministic engine authority and public-state-only presentation.
- Treat this packet as the direction lock for future art and UI work.
